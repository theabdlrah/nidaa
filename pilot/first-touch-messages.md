# Nidaa — First-Touch Messages & Interview Guide

## Objective

Secure a **15–30 minute problem-discovery conversation** — not a pilot, not a
partnership, not a product demo.

The goal of outreach is **not to validate Nidaa**. It is to **falsify our
assumptions**. We are trying to learn whether the coordination problem we
believe exists is real, urgent, and worth changing behavior for. If the first
five conversations prove Nidaa is solving the wrong problem, that is a
successful outcome — it saves us months of building the wrong thing.

Tone:
> "We are researching coordination challenges in connectivity-stressed
> environments and would value your perspective."
Not:
> "We have a solution and would like you to test it."

Every message must:
- Ask for **expertise**, not commitment.
- Not imply a pilot is expected.
- Not imply the problem is already proven.
- Acknowledge that **Nidaa may be solving the wrong problem**.
- Invite correction.

No demo link. A live screen-share is offered only if they ask — and framed as
"here's what we've built so far, in case it helps you react to it," never as a
sales artifact.

══════════════════════════════════════
INTERVIEW GUIDE — built to break our assumptions
══════════════════════════════════════
Goal of the call: leave knowing whether Nidaa's core assumptions are true,
false, or untestable. Maximize learning, not politeness. The interviewee is the
expert; you are the student. Take notes; do not defend the product.

PRINCIPLES
- Lead with curiosity. State up front: "I'm here to be corrected."
- Do not demo unless asked. If asked, show briefly and say "react honestly."
- When they agree with you, dig: "what would have to be true for this NOT to matter?"
- When they disagree, stop and ask: "what am I getting wrong?"
- Take the hardest answer as the most valuable.

────────── OPENING (2–3 min) ──────────
1. Thanks for the time. I'm researching coordination in low-connectivity
   environments. I've built a prototype, but I suspect my assumptions are wrong,
   and I'd rather hear that from you.
2. Walk me through your role and what a typical coordination day looks like.
3. What does "coordination" even mean in your context — what are you actually
   trying to make happen?

────────── PROBLEM-DISCOVERY (8–10 min) ──────────
4. When things go wrong in coordination, what specifically goes wrong?
5. Is the bottleneck INFORMATION (you don't know what exists), MATCHING
   (help exists but can't be connected), VERIFICATION (you can't trust what you
   hear), or something else entirely (supply, trust, politics, access)?
6. Tell me about the last time coordination failed. What happened? What was the
   cost?
7. Honest question: is coordination even a priority problem for you, or is it
   dwarfed by resources / access / security?
8. If you could wave a magic wand and fix ONE thing about coordination, what is it?

────────── CURRENT WORKFLOWS (5 min) ──────────
9. What tools do you use today — WhatsApp, Telegram, Airtable, Sheets, radio,
   paper, something else?
10. What works about them that you'd never give up?
11. What specifically breaks — and when (always, or only under load / disconnection)?
12. You've presumably tried other things. What did you try and abandon, and why?

────────── TRUST & VERIFICATION (5 min) ──────────
13. When someone posts "we need X at location Y," how do you decide whether to
    believe it today?
14. Who do the people you serve actually TRUST to confirm something is real?
    (A local committee? A known org? A UN body? Nobody?)
15. If I said "trusted coordinators mark entries verified," who would that
    verifier be in your context — and why would people accept them?
16. What would have to go wrong for a verification system to do harm (e.g. a
    verifier compromised, or the board used as a targeting list)?

────────── OFFLINE OPERATION (3–4 min) ──────────
17. When connectivity drops, what breaks first, and for how long?
18. Could your team still post and read needs/offers with NO internet for days?
    How do you manage that now?
19. Is "works offline" actually a deciding factor, or is it a nice-to-have you'd
    trade for something else?

────────── ASSUMPTION-FALSIFICATION (closing, 3–5 min) ──────────
20. I assumed a shared, verifiable, offline-first board would help. In what
    recent real incident would it actually have helped — and in what incidents
    would it have done nothing?
21. What would make a NEW coordination tool worth adopting over what you use now?
    (Be blunt: is the switch cost worth it?)
22. What am I getting wrong? What question should I have asked but didn't?
23. If Nidaa is solving the wrong problem, what's the right one?

────────── AFTER THE CALL ──────────
- Write 3 bullets: (a) one assumption falsified, (b) one assumption confirmed,
  (c) the single most surprising thing.
- Map their answer to Q5 (info/matching/verification/other) — this is the core
  signal for whether Nidaa's premise holds.
- Do not follow up asking for a pilot. If THEY raise it, note it and redirect to
  "let's understand the problem first."
- Share the 3 bullets back to them as a thank-you + accuracy check.

══════════════════════════════════════
THE 7 ASSUMPTIONS WE ARE TESTING
════════════════════════════════════
1. Is coordination actually a priority problem? (Q7)
2. Is the bottleneck information, matching, verification, or something else? (Q5)
3. Are WhatsApp / Telegram / Airtable already sufficient? (Q9–Q12)
4. What specifically breaks in existing workflows? (Q4, Q11)
5. Would a shared, verifiable, offline-first board have helped in any recent
   real-world incident? (Q20)
6. Who would organizations trust as verifiers? (Q14–Q15)
7. What would make a new coordination tool worth adopting? (Q21)

If conversations 1–5 falsify assumptions 1, 2, or 5, that is a win — it tells
us to stop or redirect before building further.
