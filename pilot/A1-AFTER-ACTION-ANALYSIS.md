# A1 After-Action Analysis

**Mission:** Obtain ONE first-hand after-action review (AAR) that directly addresses
operational failures and information flow, to move A1 from Weak toward Emerging
Convergence or Falsified. No new contacts, no design, no architecture, no pilots.

**Source retrieved (real, verified):** Afghanistan — After Action Review: Eastern
Region Earthquake Response (31 August 2025, Nangarhar + Kunar). Authored by the
Inter-Cluster Coordination Team (ICCT); published by UN OCHA on ReliefWeb, 21 May 2026.
URL (HTTP 200 verified): https://reliefweb.int/report/afghanistan/afghanistan-after-action-review-eastern-region-earthquake-response-21-may-2026
Method: document review, operational data analysis, key-informant interviews, focus
groups, field consultations with humanitarian actors, de facto authorities, and
affected communities (incl. Afghan women responders), Jan–Mar 2026.

---

## Context

- Real response: 31 Aug 2025 earthquake, eastern Afghanistan (Nangarhar/Kunar).
- By January 2026, >300,000 people received assistance (>230,000 in priority districts).
- The ICCT self-initiated an AAR to assess the response across operational coordination,
  response delivery, **information management**, logistics, access, and accountability.
- ReliefWeb tags it under "Themes: **Coordination**", "Recovery and Reconstruction",
  disaster type Earthquake.

## Operational failure

The AAR's headline finding on the early phase:

> "The most significant early constraint was **not a lack of humanitarian capacity, but the
> limited operational readiness of first 72-hour activation and sequencing arrangements**
> needed to turn preparedness plans into immediate field action. While contingency planning
> had intended to frontload key operational decisions, some elements were re-discussed and
> partially re-developed during the initial response phase, **contributing to avoidable delays.**"

A second failure was information/decision quality:

> "Early planning figures generated through the Disaster Impact Model (DIM) were constrained by
> **outdated shelter typology data and inaccuracies in modelled earthquake intensity data**,
> limiting confidence in projected damage estimates during the initial phase."

## Root cause(s)

- Not insufficient actors or funding — **activation/sequencing readiness** failed to convert
  existing plans into field action fast enough.
- **Information-management weakness**: planning depended on stale/incorrect data, undermining
  early damage estimates and therefore early prioritization.
- The review notes "persistent structural gaps" despite improved readiness vs the 2023 Herat
  response — i.e. coordination readiness improved but did not close.

## Was coordination involved?

**Yes — explicitly.** The AAR lists "operational coordination" and "information management" as
primary reviewed dimensions, and the binding early constraint (activation/sequencing of the
coordination architecture) is itself a coordination failure, not a logistics or access failure.

## Was coordination primary, secondary, or irrelevant?

**Primary (for the early-phase binding constraint).** The AAR's single "most significant early
constraint" was a coordination/activation-sequencing failure. Capacity and access existed; the
failure was in *coordinating the turn-on* of the response and in the *quality of the information*
used to sequence it. This is precisely A1's claim: when operations fail, coordination is
frequently a binding constraint.

(Caveat: this is one response, and it ultimately delivered at scale — coordination was a
*constraint that caused avoidable delay*, not a total breakdown. That nuance matters for A1's
strength, not its direction.)

## Impact on A1

A1 = "When operations fail, coordination is frequently a binding constraint."

- This AAR is **first-hand, published, primary-field evidence** that in a real response the
  binding early constraint WAS coordination/activation-sequencing (and information management),
  not capacity. It directly supports A1.
- It does NOT falsify A1; it strengthens it. But it is a single response — convergence requires
  repetition (per the project's rule: 3 independent sources agreeing = strong).
- It also validates the *information-management* angle (stale data → bad early decisions), which
  reinforces A2/A3 adjacency without overclaiming.

## Comparison against existing evidence

- **Adam interview (resident, Gaza):** did NOT raise coordination as a top pain; raised
  info-access during connectivity outages (A2/A3). The AAR and Adam are not contradictory — Adam
  is a *beneficiary/resident* view (info flow to people); the AAR is a *responder/coordinator*
  view (info + activation flow among actors). They operate at different layers. The AAR actually
  supplies the coordinator-layer signal Adam could not.
- **Abdelrahman reply (Wa7at, Gaza engineer):** flagged internet/electricity scarcity. Consistent
  with the AAR's info-management theme — when the information base is degraded (here: stale data;
  there: connectivity loss), coordination suffers.
- **Existing public evidence (HDX HAPI / OCHA clusters):** showed coordination *exists* as
  infrastructure. The AAR shows that infrastructure can still be the *binding early constraint*
  when activation/sequencing readiness is limited. This moves A1 from "coordination exists" to
  "coordination failure was the named early constraint" — a qualitative step.

## Verdict

**Based on current evidence, A1 is: Strengthened.**

Why (≤10 lines): One real, first-hand AAR (Afghanistan 2025 earthquake, ICCT/OCHA) names
coordination/activation-sequencing as the single most significant early constraint — not
capacity. That is direct, primary-field support for A1. It is one source, so convergence is not
yet established (still below the 3-independent-sources bar). Adam's resident view is complementary,
not contradictory. A1 moves from Weak toward Emerging Convergence, but needs 1–2 more independent
coordinator-level accounts (or AARs) to cross the threshold. No design work proceeds until then.
