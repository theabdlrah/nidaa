## B. Five targeted questions (focus: ownership / maintenance / verification / conflict / survival under pressure)

1. **Information ownership** — "When a rescue or incident happens, who is responsible for recording it? Is
   there a named person or role that owns the field report, or does it get captured ad hoc by whoever is
   there?"
2. **Data maintenance** — "What does the recorded information actually look like — a structured field report
   / register / database, or notes that live in chat? How often is it updated, and does that slip when
   operations surge?"
3. **Source verification** — "Before an incident count or casualty figure is shared externally, how is it
   checked? Who decides a number is trustworthy enough to publish?"
4. **Conflicting information** — "When field teams report different versions of the same event, how is the
   discrepancy resolved? Is there one agreed source, or does conflicting info circulate until someone settles it?"
5. **Structured-information survival under pressure** — "During the worst periods — heavy bombardment,
   connectivity blackout, or a surge of incidents — does the structured reporting keep going, or does it
   collapse back to calls and chat? What keeps it alive (or kills it)?"

**Why these five:** they map directly onto the Six A6 Questions (Q1 ownership, Q2 medium, Q3 what's written,
Q4 what's verbal, Q6 what's abandoned) with explicit emphasis on verification (Q3 here) and conflicting-info
resolution (the exact pain Mones named in Q10: "one clear primary source + a trusted backup"). If Hisem
answers that a named role owns structured field reports that survive bombardment via a mandated reporting
chain, White Helmets is the clean A6b SUCCESS case at the semi-formal tier — which would close the
owner/mandate branch definitively and sharpen the remaining unknown to the LOOSE-INFORMAL branch only.
