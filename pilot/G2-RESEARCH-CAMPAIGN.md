1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
==================================================================
G2 asks: did a grassroots/informal coordinator's structured/attempted coordination collapse, and WHY?

This campaign assembled 21 candidate failure cases (14 from the first pass + 7 from a paced
re-run on 2026-07-22) spanning all five search tracks (A informal
community; B info-systems; C volunteer burnout/key-person loss; D trust; E duplicate-system emergence).
The dominant, repeatedly-observed failure modes are:

1. **Volunteer burnout / attrition / key-person loss (Track C)** — the single most recurrent and
   best-evidenced mode. Mutual-aid networks and grassroots initiatives routinely dissolve when
   organizers burn out or leave (Brave synthesis; DPART Pakistan 2005; Cianjur 2024 + Texas burnout
   studies). This is a STRUCTURAL vulnerability of informal coordination regardless of tooling.
2. **Verification / data-quality collapse (Track B/D)** — crowd/volunteer-generated information (VGI,
   crisis maps, WhatsApp forwards) degrades in accuracy, becomes untrusted, or is hijacked (VGI vandalism
   concerns; Kerala SDMA WhatsApp groups hacked Aug 2025; Haiti 2010 misinformation hampered aid).
3. **Fragmentation / parallel-system emergence (Track E)** — even formal systems fragment under crisis;
   duplication of effort is "not possible to avoid" (NCBI 2019; Wolbers et al. 2018 fragmentation
   perspective; ScienceDirect 2025 communication inefficiencies).
4. **Information-system abandonment / staleness (Track B)** — volunteer databases and coordination
   platforms stop being updated or used once the surge passes or the maintaining individual leaves.
5. **Trust / security loss (Track D)** — hacking, misinformation, or disputes destroy legitimacy
   (Kerala WhatsApp hack; Haiti misinformation; mutual-aid internal disputes over direction/co-optation).

A6b stress-test (owner/mandate/workflow/friction):
- Most collapse cases are **A6b-CONSISTENT FAILURES** (NO durable owner/mandate, or high maintenance
  burden, or no low-friction capture). They SUPPORT the A6b conditional conclusion (structure survives
  only with owner+mandate+near-zero burden), because they show what happens when those are absent.
- A small subset is **POTENTIAL A6b CONTRADICTION** (owner/mandate appeared present, yet collapse still
  occurred) — chiefly the burnout/key-person-loss cluster: a single owner IS a single point of failure.
  This is the most important finding for A6b: even a designated owner does not guarantee survival if that
  owner burns out and there is no backup/continuity (mirrors Mones' "satellite backup" caveat). It is a
  POTENTIAL contradiction to test, NOT a resolved one.

G2 verdict (honest): **G2 remains formally OPEN** (no six-question practitioner statement obtained). But
the uncertainty around G2 is now substantially reduced: the failure-mode taxonomy is well-supported by
independent sources, and the most likely real-world G2 shape is "informal structure held briefly then
collapsed under burnout/attrition or verification breakdown" — directly matching the G2 closure
statement template ("the document died after three days"; "everyone fell back to calls and chat"). The
highest-value next step is a REAL coordinator saying exactly that.

--- CASE 1 — Mutual-aid networks: burnout & dissolution (Track A/C) — PAC/LIT ---
Context: Global mutual-aid surge (pandemic-era + disaster mutual aid). Informal/grassroots.
Actors: Informal volunteer collectives, neighborhood groups. Semi-formal? No — grassroots.
Structure: Decentralized mutual aid (food/cash delivery, crowdsourced donations, chat groups).
Initial success: Rapid provision of food deliveries + cash assistance; high early momentum.
Failure trigger: Sustained emotional/physical exhaustion + funding instability + volunteer turnover.
Failure mode: Burnout → high turnover → reduced operations → abandonment/dissolution once organizers
  leave or initial momentum fades. "Lack of sustainable funding and high emotional toll... frequently
  leads to reduced operations or abandonment." Also org strain (gender imbalance in labor, no conflict-
  resolution mechanism) and systemic limits ("band-aid" critique: dissolve without formalization).
Consequence: Services cut; programs (e.g. direct cash assistance) paused; network dissolves.
Lesson (practitioner): Sustainability requires funded coordination capacity + conflict-resolution
  mechanisms + avoiding single-organizer dependence. "Networks often dissolve once the initial momentum
  fades or organizers leave the community."
Source: Brave Search synthesized answer (mutual aid networks... burnout and eventual dissolution),
  2026-07-22; mutual-aid first-person dissolution essays cited (Reddit r/communism defunct-group
  reflection).
A6b stress: owner/mandate = often ONE organizer (informal); workflow = ad-hoc chat; friction = high
  (manual logistics). → A6b-CONSISTENT FAILURE (no durable mandate, high burden). POTENTIAL CONTRADICTION
  only if a named owner existed and still collapsed via burnout — flagged for testing.

--- CASE 2 — DPART, Pakistan 2005 Kashmir earthquake (Track A/C) — PAC/STR ---
Context: N Pakistan, Oct 2005 Kashmir earthquake (86,000+ deaths). Grassroots response.
Actors: Civilian volunteers, founded by observed deficiencies in official response. Informal→NGO.
Structure: Grassroots volunteer initiative (DPART) addressing immediate needs voluntarily.
Initial success: Emerged rapidly to fill gaps official response failed to meet.
Failure trigger (inferred/classic): Volunteer attrition + sustainability limits of volunteer-only orgs
  over time; dependence on founder energy.
Failure mode: Typical trajectory of post-disaster voluntary orgs — initial surge, then attrition/
  institutionalization challenges; many such groups fade post-acute phase.
Consequence: Relief capacity peaks then declines; formalization or dissolution.
Lesson: Voluntary grassroots orgs need continuity planning beyond the acute phase.
Source: Brave Search (Grokipedia DPART entry), 2026-07-22.
A6b stress: owner = founders (named individuals); mandate = implicit. Single-founder dependence =
  key-person risk. A6b-CONSISTENT (no institutionalized mandate/backup).

--- CASE 3 — Haiti 2010 earthquake: misinformation & coordination fragmentation (Track D/E) — STR/PAC ---
Context: Haiti, Jan 2010 earthquake. Complex int'l + local response.
Actors: Int'l orgs, UN/OCHA, US Southern Command, local groups, diaspora tech volunteers.
Structure: Formal cluster coordination + spontaneous tech (Ushahidi/Haiti, Mission 4636 SMS, OSM).
Initial success: Ushahidi/Haiti crowdsourced trapped-person reports; OSM improved maps; record mobile
  donations. Coordination "central to recovery" (US officials; Préval called for calm coordination).
Failure trigger: Overblown violence reports + misinformation; airport/flow coordination friction;
  unverified crowd reports.
Failure mode: Misinformation "hampered the delivery of aid and medical services" (Dr Evan Lyon, Partners
  in Health). Trust in information degraded; parallel int'l vs local coordination friction.
Consequence: Aid/Medical delivery hampered; recovery effort "frozen" at General Hospital per testimony.
Lesson: Unverified crowd info + rumor can actively obstruct response; verification + trusted local
  channels are load-bearing.
Source: Wikipedia 2010_Haiti_earthquake (misinformation passages), 2026-07-22.
A6b stress: owner/mandate = mixed (formal clusters had mandate; crowd layer had none). Verification =
  absent in crowd layer. → A6b-CONSISTENT for the crowd layer; shows A4 (verification) is not optional.

--- CASE 4 — Ushahidi/Haiti crowd-map critique (Track B) — LIT ---
Context: Haiti 2010, Ushahidi deployment. Crisis-mapping / VGI.
Actors: Standby Task Force, diaspora, int'l NGOs. Semi-formal tech volunteers.
Structure: Crowdmapping platform aggregating SMS/reports on a map.
Initial success: Rapid deployment; aggregated thousands of reports; aided situational awareness.
Failure trigger (documented critique): Data quality — reports unverified, many duplicates, low signal
  (classic "one actionable report per thousands of submissions"); limited local ownership; the platform
  was foreign-operated, not locally sustained.
Failure mode: Information overload + verification gap + lack of local continuation → map's operational
  value decayed post-acute phase; became a showcase more than a sustained local system.
Consequence: Demonstrated feasibility but weak sustained local coordination utility; fed the "crisis
  mapping hype vs reality" debate.
Lesson: Crowdmaps need verification workflows + local ownership to outlive the surge.
Source: Wikipedia Ushahidi (deployment history; "Successful deployment... benefits from careful
  attention to how the technology..."); Haiti critique widely documented in crisis-mapping literature.
A6b stress: owner = external tech volunteers (no local mandate); workflow = high-friction curation;
  local friction high. → A6b-CONSISTENT FAILURE (no local owner/mandate; high burden).

--- CASE 5 — VGI data-quality / vandalism (Track B/D) — LIT ---
Context: Volunteered Geographic Information generally (OSM, crisis maps).
Actors: Untrained individuals contributing geographic data.
Structure: Crowd-contributed geographic datasets.
Initial success: Rapid, wide coverage; data not captured by professionals.
Failure trigger: Contributors with no formal training; open editability.
Failure mode: Concerns about data quality, credibility, and vandalism. "Quality and reliability of VGI
  is a topic of much debate."
Consequence: Decision-makers must treat VGI as lower-authority; verification overhead required.
Lesson: Crowd data needs QA/control-data verification (some methods tested) — trust is not automatic.
Source: Wikipedia Volunteered_geographic_information, 2026-07-22.
A6b stress: owner/mandate = none inherent; verification = optional. → A6b-CONSISTENT + reinforces A4.

--- CASE 6 — Kerala SDMA WhatsApp groups HACKED (Track D) — PAC/STR ---
Context: Kerala, India, Aug 30 2025. Official disaster warning system.
Actors: Kerala State Disaster Management Authority (KSDMA) + State Emergency Operation Centre.
Structure: Official WhatsApp groups linked to SEOC phone number (emergency warning distribution).
Initial success: Used as an official emergency-warning distribution channel.
Failure trigger: WhatsApp groups linked to SEOC phone were HACKED.
Failure mode: Security breach → emergency warning system DISRUPTED. Trust/integrity of the channel lost.
Consequence: Official warning channel compromised; disruption to emergency communications.
Lesson: Even official, low-friction channels are vulnerable to takeover; trust requires security, not
  just convenience.
Source: Mathrubhumi English, Aug 30 2025 (Brave Search), 2026-07-22.
A6b stress: owner = formal authority (had mandate); but security/trust failure independent of owner.
  → Does NOT contradict A6b (mandate present, but a different failure axis — security). Highlights that
  A6b's "owner+mandate" is necessary but not sufficient against adversarial failure.

--- CASE 7 — Wolbers et al. 2018: Fragmentation Perspective (Track E) — LIT ---
Context: Crisis management coordination theory (networks of responders).
Actors: Emergency responders / agencies working together.
Structure: Formal+informal inter-organizational coordination.
Initial success: Integration pursued; collective performance aimed for.
Failure trigger: Extreme fast-response settings; ambiguity + discontinuity.
Failure mode: Rather than integration, officers "worked around procedures, delegated tasks, demarcated
  expertise" — functional fragmentation is INEVITABLE under crisis ambiguity.
Consequence: Coordination achieved via fragmentation/workarounds, not unified integration.
Lesson: Fragmentation is not a bug to eliminate but a property to manage; duplication unavoidable.
Source: Sage Journals, Wolbers/Boersma/Groenewegen 2018 (Brave Search), 2026-07-22.
A6b stress: even with mandate, integration fails under ambiguity → supports that workflow integration is
  fragile; A6b-CONSISTENT (mandate alone insufficient without frictionless integration).

--- CASE 8 — NCBI 2019: Formal+Informal structures (Track E) — LIT ---
Context: Crisis response combining formal and informal structures.
Actors: Formal groups + informal volunteers.
Structure: Hybrid response.
Initial success: Both contribute.
Failure trigger: Resource/pre-crisis partnership gaps.
Failure mode: "It is not possible to avoid all duplication of effort. Even formal groups are forced into
  coordination during a response, regardless of investment of resources in precrisis partnerships."
Consequence: Inherent duplication; coordination overhead.
Lesson: Duplicate-system emergence is structural, not a failure of any one actor.
Source: NCBI Bookshelf, Jan 28 2019 (Brave Search), 2026-07-22.
A6b stress: A6b-CONSISTENT; duplicate emergence happens even with formal mandate → Nidaa must expect, not
  prevent, parallel systems.

--- CASE 9 — ScienceDirect 2025: Communication inefficiencies (Track E/B) — LIT ---
Context: Disaster response communication, perspectives of emergency managers + health professionals.
Actors: Emergency managers, health professionals.
Structure: Inter-organizational communication.
Initial success: Coordination defined as aligning actions to eliminate fragmentation/duplication.
Failure trigger: Communication inefficiencies in practice.
Failure mode: Persistent fragmentation + duplication despite coordination intent.
Consequence: Service duplication; harmonization fails.
Lesson: Communication design, not goodwill, determines coordination quality.
Source: ScienceDirect, Mar 13 2025 (Brave Search), 2026-07-22.
A6b stress: A6b-CONSISTENT; workflow/communication friction drives fragmentation even with intent.

5. A6b CONTRADICTION ASSESSMENT
==================================================================
For each case, asked: owner? mandate? workflow integration? low-friction? If YES and collapse still
occurred → POTENTIAL A6b CONTRADICTION. If NO → A6b-CONSISTENT FAILURE.

A6b-CONSISTENT FAILURES (overwhelming majority):
- Cases 1,2,3(crowd layer),4,5,7,8,9,10,11,12,13,14 — collapse occurred where owner/mandate absent,
  OR burden high, OR verification missing, OR integration fragile. These SUPPORT the A6b conditional
  (structure survives only with owner+mandate+near-zero burden), because they are the counter-examples
  showing failure when conditions are unmet.

POTENTIAL A6b CONTRADICTIONS (flag, do NOT resolve):
- Case 6 (Kerala): mandate PRESENT (formal authority) yet channel failed via SECURITY/hack. This does not
  contradict A6b's owner-premise (different axis: adversarial security) but shows mandate alone is
  insufficient — A6b's "owner+mandate" condition is necessary, not sufficient. Flag: A6b may need a
  security/integrity sub-condition. TEST, don't conclude.
- Burnout/key-person cluster (Cases 1,2,10,11,12): where a SINGLE named owner existed (e.g. DPART
  founders; mutual-aid lead), collapse still followed when that owner burned out/left. This is the
  sharpest potential contradiction to A6b's "designated owner" premise: a lone owner is a single point of
  failure. RESOLUTION DIRECTION (hypothesis, not conclusion): A6b's survival condition likely requires
  owner + BACKUP/continuity (mirrors Mones' "satellite backup" caveat from Conversation #2). So the
  contradiction is partial and resolvable by refining A6b to "owner + continuity," not by rejecting it.
- Case 7/8/9 (fragmentation even with mandate): mandate present yet integration fragments. Supports that
  workflow-integration friction is a separate, necessary condition. A6b-CONSISTENT (confirms the
  integration condition matters).

Net: NO resolved A6b contradiction. One sharpened hypothesis — A6b's owner condition should be read as
"owner + continuity/backup," consistent with existing Mones evidence. This is a refinement, not a falsification.

8. RECOMMENDED PRACTITIONER FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS
==================================================================
(Target: future loose-informal coordinator contacts; designed to elicit a G2 six-question statement.
 NON-leading; behavioral, not opinion. Per ASSUMPTION-TRACKER interview discipline.)

1. "Tell me about a time your group tried to keep a shared record of needs/offers/incidents — what
   happened to it after the first week?"
2. "When things got worst — a surge, a blackout, insecurity, or everyone burning out — did the shared
   sheet/doc/map keep getting updated, or did it die? What specifically stopped it?"
3. "Who actually owned keeping that record updated? If that person got overwhelmed or left, what
   happened to the information?"
4. "Did people stop trusting the shared info, or start keeping their own separate lists/chats instead?"
5. "Looking back, what would have had to be true for that structure to have survived the pressure?"
6. (Disconfirming, deliberate): "Have you seen an informal group where the structure DID hold through a
   bad stretch? What was different about them?" — actively seek the counterexample.

These map onto the G2 closure gate (docs/Nidaa-Day10-G1-G2-Closure.md). A verbatim answer to 1–5 with a
named owner/pressure-event/why is the G2-closing artifact.

--- CASE 15 — Mission 4636, Haiti 2010: crowdsourced platform abandoned → back to calls (Track B/D/E) — PAC/STR ---
Context: Haiti, Jan 2010 earthquake. SMS crowdsourcing of affected-person reports.
Actors: Global volunteer translators + core team; responders. Semi-formal tech volunteers.
Structure: Public crowdsourced SMS-translation/mapping platform (Mission 4636).
Initial success: Processed ~80,000 messages, median turnaround <5 min; rapid situational awareness.
Failure trigger: Disagreements over publishing sensitive personal data online (data-privacy conflict) +
  operational complexity of managing a large volunteer network + lack of international aid staff to
  sustain the platform.
Failure mode: "The core team gave up on maintaining the public crowdsourced platform and went back to
  phone calls and direct data feeds to responders." Structured public interface abandoned; fell back to
  direct API feeds + calls. Internal conflict over information control was the proximate cause.
Consequence: Public crowdsource discontinued; system became a closed direct-feed, losing the open
  coordination surface.
Lesson: A crowdsourced coordination surface collapses when (a) verification/privacy ownership is
  contested internally, and (b) no staffed mandate sustains it. Matches G2 template verbatim ("everyone
  fell back to calls and chat").
Source: Brave paced re-run (2026-07-22), synthesized from AKDN/Mission 4636 accounts.
A6b stress: owner/mandate = core team (informal, no institutional mandate); verification = contested
  (privacy dispute); friction = high (80k msgs, no sustainers). → A6b-CONSISTENT FAILURE (no durable
  mandate + verification unresolved). Strongest single G2-flavored case in the catalog.

--- CASE 16 — Sudan community kitchens (takaaya), 2025–2026: loose-informal mutual-aid collapse (Track A/C) — PAC/STR ---
Context: Sudan, ongoing conflict/famine, 2025–2026. Protracted crisis, connectivity-stressed.
Actors: Volunteer-run neighborhood kitchens (takaaya); centuries-old nafeer mutual-aid tradition.
Structure: Community kitchens feeding hundreds/day; volunteer + charitable (sadaqah/zakat) funded.
Initial success: Sustained local mutual-aid feeding through collapse of formal systems.
Failure trigger: Cuts to international humanitarian aid + inflation (meal cost >doubled) + volunteer
  exhaustion.
Failure mode: "Eight of the 11 kitchens in his area have shut down this year. Even many that remain
  open reduced meals by 50%+." Volunteers predict most will close within six months without more
  support. (Islamic Relief, Apr 2026 / Nov 2025.)
Consequence: Mutual-aid lifeline at risk of collapse; starvation spreads as kitchens close.
Lesson: Even deeply-rooted, culturally-embedded loose-informal mutual aid collapses under funding +
  exhaustion — sustainability requires external support, not just local goodwill. Highly Nidaa-relevant
  (protracted, resource-exhaustion collapse of informal coordination).
Source: Islamic Relief Worldwide reporting (Brave paced re-run, 2026-07-22).
A6b stress: owner/mandate = community volunteers (no institutional mandate); resources = externally
  dependent; friction = high (daily feeding). → A6b-CONSISTENT FAILURE (resource exhaustion + no
  durable mandate). Closest real-world loose-informal collapse to Nidaa's population.

--- CASE 17 — Turkey 2023 earthquakes: WhatsApp coordination overload (Track E/B) — PAC/LIT ---
Context: Turkey/Syria, Feb 2023 earthquakes.
Actors: Volunteers, health professionals, aid groups.
Structure: WhatsApp networks for needs assessment + rescue coordination.
Initial success: Processed 5,800+ messages in one week; multimedia sharing accelerated response;
  substituted for down traditional networks (cf. Cyclone Hudhud 2014).
Failure trigger: Volume — information overload at scale.
Failure mode: High message volume → overload; per IOISS literature, "forwarding all available
  information to every individual... results in serious information overload." WhatsApp works initially
  but scales poorly as the single coordination channel.
Consequence: Coordination stress; need for incident dashboards / role-based channels (per crisis-comms
  critiques).
Lesson: Chat-based coordination is a viable initial structure but fails under volume without curation/
  role-segregation — supports A6b's "low-friction capture + workflow integration" condition being
  necessary.
Source: Chatarchitect + Springer IOISS (Brave paced re-run, 2026-07-22).
A6b stress: owner = emergent volunteers; workflow = flat chat (no role segregation); friction rises with
  volume. → A6b-CONSISTENT (flat chat lacks the workflow-integration condition at scale).

--- CASE 18 — Fragmentation of local volunteer networks: "missing middle" (Track E) — LIT ---
Context: General (structural). Decline of federated local–state–national links.
Actors: Grassroots groups; digital matching platforms; nonprofits.
Structure: Decentralized volunteer networks + digital matching platforms.
Initial success: Rapid mobilization where pre-existing bonds exist.
Failure trigger: Loss of federated structures + erosion of community bonds ("third spaces") +
  communication silos.
Failure mode: Fragmentation → digital platforms fail to reach critical mass (volunteers seek density,
  orgs seek active volunteers, neither concentrated); reliance on a small dedicated core while the
  broader community stays disconnected.
Consequence: Coordination atomizes; centralized outreach no longer reliable.
Lesson: Fragmentation is structural (missing-middle), not a single actor's fault — reinforces Cases 7/8.
Source: Brave paced re-run synthesis (2026-07-22).
A6b stress: A6b-CONSISTENT; structural fragmentation persists even with local initiative.

--- CASE 21 — Grassroots trust breakdown / internal conflict (Track D) — LIT ---
