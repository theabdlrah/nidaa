The phenomenon where a spontaneous community response or behavior withers after initial enthusiasm is primarily described by the concepts of extinction and spontaneous recovery in behavioral psychology, or by historical accounts of organizational fatigue. 

Behavioral Psychology Perspective

Extinction: In classical and operant conditioning, extinction refers to the gradual weakening and disappearance of a conditioned response when the reinforcement (or unconditioned stimulus) is no longer presented. 
 For example, if a behavior is no longer rewarded, it eventually ceases.

Spontaneous Recovery: This is the sudden appearance of a response after a period of extinction. 
 It demonstrates that the original learning association is suppressed, not eliminated, and can reappear after a rest period, though typically with less intensity. 

Organizational and Social Context

Spontaneous Loss of Enthusiasm: Historical labor movements, such as those involving the International Workers of the World (IWW), have documented tactics or outcomes described as a "spontaneous loss of enthusiasm," where initial high energy in community or workplace actions dissipates without structural reinforcement or continued engagement. 

Emergent Groups: Research on emergent groups and spontaneous volunteers in disaster response notes that while initial altruism and cohesion are strong, these self-organizing groups often face challenges in long-term sustainability and integration with formal planning, which can lead to the withering of their impact after the initial crisis phase.
