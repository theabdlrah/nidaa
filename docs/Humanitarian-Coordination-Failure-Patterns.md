TOP 10 RECURRING COORDINATION FAILURE PATTERNS
==================================================================

P1. Local actors excluded from decision-making / "localisation gap"
- TYPE: Governance-related (also Trust-related)
- EVIDENCE:
  * ICVA (2026-10-22), "Adapting humanitarian coordination –
    Challenges, innovations, and future directions for NGO-led mechanisms"
    — analyses NGO-led coordination in Myanmar, Sudan, Nigeria, Syria and
    their implications for response-level coordination.
  * Grand Bargain (2016) localisation commitments + subsequent
    evaluations (El-Jardali et al., K2P Center / ALNAP GHA lineage):
    repeated finding that pledged lead-role for local actors is not realised
    in coordination architectures.
  * Elkahlout & Milton, "Localization of Humanitarian Action in
    Gaza as a Field Imperative" — local actors sidelined even where they
    are the primary responders.
- REAL-WORLD EX: Syria cross-border aid coordination; Gaza 2023–
  present (local/international division of labour in coordination).
- CONF: HIGH

P2. Information fragmentation across siloed systems
- TYPE: Information-related (also Communication-related)
- EVIDENCE:
  * "Humanitarian information management" literature (OpenAlex-indexed
    works on coordination/information management — could not be re-fetched this
    session; carried from prior verification of the MENA health-systems
    research cohort).
  * ALNAP GHA Report 2026 — system-level reporting shows persistent
    gaps in shared, comparable operational data across responders.
- REAL-WORLD EX: post-earthquake Syria (2023) — multiple overlapping
  needs-assessment and reporting systems, low interoperability.
- CONF: MED

P3. Trust deficit between local and international actors
- TYPE: Trust-related
- EVIDENCE:
  * ICVA (2026-10-22) — NGO-led mechanisms study surfaces
    trust/funding-power asymmetries as a coordination barrier.
  * El Achi et al., "Health aid displacement during a decade of
    conflict in Syria" (BMC Public Health) — aid harmonisation failure
    rooted in actor mistrust and competition.
- REAL-WORLD EX: Syria health-cluster harmonisation failures (2011–2019).
- CONF: HIGH

P4. Cluster/system coordination fails to reach frontline responders
- TYPE: Governance-related (also Communication-related)
- EVIDENCE:
  * OCHA cluster-system critiques (via ICVA 2026 + localisation lit) —
    coordination architectures designed at capital/headquarters level do not
    reliably extend to community-level responders.
  * Ground Truth Solutions Ebola perception studies (2026-06-09) — affected
    populations' concerns not fed back into response design.
- REAL-WORLD EX: Ebola (Ituri) — community perceptions documented but
  not operationally integrated; Nepal drought 2025 — first-time response
  operation by Nepal Red Cross flagged coordination learning gaps.
- CONF: MED

P5. After-action learning is captured but not fed back / not implemented
- TYPE: Governance-related (also Information-related)
- EVIDENCE:
  * IFRC / Nepal Red Cross (2026-11-06), "Nepal drought response
    2025: Lessons, learning and reflection workshop report" — explicit
    lessons-learned artifact, the existence of which implies prior gaps
    recurring (reflection needed because failure repeated).
  * ALNAP GHA 2026 + ALNAP evaluation library (24,958 resources) —
    large body of evaluations/lessons that nonetheless coincides with
    repeated coordination failure (volume of lessons ≠ uptake).
- REAL-WORLD EX: Nepal 2025 drought/cholera (first drought response
  operation → learning workshop needed).
- CONF: HIGH

P6. Resource/en透明度 asymmetry distorts coordination
- TYPE: Resource-related (also Governance-related)
- EVIDENCE:
  * ICVA (2026-10-22) — funding-power asymmetry between
    international NGOs and local actors shapes who coordinates.
  * Center for Disaster Philanthropy, "The Ebola outbreak: How funders
    can support locally led response" (2026-06-22) — funder behaviour
    as a coordination determinant.
- REAL-WORLD EX: Ebola outbreak financing; locally-led response
  under-resourced relative to international lead.
- CONF: MED

P7. Community/recipient feedback loops absent or decorative
- TYPE: Communication-related (also Trust-related)
- EVIDENCE:
  * Ground Truth Solutions Ebola studies (2026-06-09) — "Perceptions,
    suggestions and fears of people living at the centre of the Ebola
    outbreak" — documents that affected-population voice is collected yet
    response design per the studies does not reflect it.
  * ALNAP GHA 2026 — accountability-to-affected-population gaps
    persist at system level.
- REAL-WORLD EX: Ebola (Ituri province) — perceptions captured,
  not acted upon in operational coordination.
- CONF: MED

P8. Ethical/accountability dilemmas unresolved in the moment
- TYPE: Governance-related (also Trust-related)
- EVIDENCE:
  * Humanitarian Outcomes / UK Humanitarian Innovation Hub (2026-05-29),
    "No easy choice: A humanitarian's guide to ethical, principled
    decision making" — a structured-dialogue tool exists BECAUSE
    coordinators hit unresolvable ethics/accountability tensions in real
    time without mechanism to resolve them.
- REAL-WORLD EX: general — the tool's existence is evidence the failure
  mode (no in-time accountability mechanism) is recurring enough to
  warrant a dedicated instrument.
- CONF: MED

P9. Aid displacement / harmonisation failure under competition
- TYPE: Trust-related (also Governance-related)
- EVIDENCE:
  * El Achi et al., "Health aid displacement during a decade of
    conflict in Syria" (BMC Public Health, 2023) — quantifies
    aid moving AWAY from need as actors compete rather than coordinate.
  * El Achi et al., "An analysis of humanitarian and health aid
    harmonisation over a decade (2011–2019) of the Syrian conflict"
    — coordination/harmonisation failure measured directly.
- REAL-WORLD EX: Syria 2011–2019 health response.
- CONF: HIGH

P10. Coordination mechanism designed for stability, brittle under surge/collapse
- TYPE: Governance-related (also Resource-related)
- EVIDENCE:
  * IFRC/Nepal Red Cross (2026-11-06) — first-time response
    operation exposed that standing coordination capacity was not in place
    for a novel hazard (drought, not flood/quake).
  * ICVA (2026-10-22) — mechanisms analysed across Myanmar/Sudan/
    Nigeria/Syria imply context-specific fragility of NGO-led coordination.
  * Nidaa field context (Gaza) — connectivity-stressed, high-surge
    environments where formal coordination erodes fastest (per the Nidaa
    A6b problem frame; this is the live-case anchor, not a published
    eval).
- REAL-WORLD EX: Nepal 2025 drought (novel hazard, no standing
  coordination); Gaza 2023–present (surge + connectivity collapse).
- CONF: MED

LINKAGE TO NIDAA A6b (why this research exists)
==================================================================
This failure-pattern study is NOT adjacent to Nidaa — it is the
failure-mode literature that justifies A6b's design bets. Nidaa A6b
(conditional survival: ownership + low burden + embedded workflow) targets
Gaza humanitarian coordination where FORMAL coordination collapses (G2 =
informal coordination collapse). The 10 patterns map onto the Nidaa
problem frame as follows:

P10 (mechanism brittle under surge/collapse) -> THE ANCHOR. Gaza =
connectivity collapse + high surge -> formal coordination erodes first.
Nidaa's offline-first / low-bandwidth / embedded-workflow design is a
direct response to P10.

P1 (local excluded) + P6 (resource asymmetry) -> why Gaza coordination is
externally dominated; Nidaa's local-first ownership model is the inverse.

P3 (trust deficit local<->intl) + P9 (aid displacement under competition)
-> the G2 mechanism: when trust fails, actors compete, aid moves off-need.
Nidaa's G2 hypothesis = P3 + P9.

P2 (info fragmentation) + P7 (feedback loops absent) -> the data-layer
failure Nidaa addresses with one shared, offline-reconciling board +
community signal.

P4 (cluster fails to reach frontline) + P5 (lessons not implemented) ->
why top-down coordination misses Nidaa's users; embedded-workflow design
targets P4.

P8 (ethics/accountability unresolved) -> governance risk in any coord
tool; relevant to Nidaa's consent/ownership model.

Net: the 10 patterns are LITERATURE SUPPORT for why a lightweight,
offline-first, locally-owned board is WORTH INVESTIGATING where formal
coordination fails. They justify the investigation; they do NOT validate
A6b. The mapping (P10=anchor, P2/P7=info layer, P3/P9=G2 mechanism) is a
HYPOTHESIS, not evidence.

THE DECISIVE SENTENCE: G1 (informal success) and G2 (informal collapse)
are the two outcomes the patterns predict. Which one holds is NOT settled
by this document — it requires field evidence: a real coordinator telling
an actual story. Named pending live-gate interviews: Hisem, Omar, Mones,
Steve Sosebee, Ekzayez, or another coordinator.
